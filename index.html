Game Day Playbook
📖 Ch. 10 — Physical Prep
"Meet their physical needs first: sleep, food, water, equipment, arrive on time. These create the container for everything else."
📖 Ch. 10 — Mental Prep
"Don't let your words contribute to the pressure they already feel. Ask: 'Is anything I'm doing or saying right now getting you more worried?'"
Stay lighthearted. Find ways to make them laugh. Play music they want on the drive over. You're not giving a pump-up talk — you're lowering the temperature.
📖 Ch. 10 — Normalize Nerves
"Nerves are what we feel facing the unknown. Anxiety comes from the fear that when the opportunity comes, we will fail. You play a role in which one wins."
Try this: "I get it. I used to get nervous too. Nerves are completely normal — God created us to get a little nervous. Whatever happens, I love you and I'm excited to watch you today."
🙏 Prayer Framework (Ch. 10)
Pray for them to play, not perform. To have fun. To respect coaches and officials. To be a great teammate. To honor their opponent. To learn something new about themselves. To see people who go unnoticed.
📖 Mantras for Game Day
Audience of One. With God for God. See the Unseen.
📖 Pregame Verses (Ch. 10)
"Whatever you do, work heartily, as for the Lord and not for men." — Colossians 3:23
"Be strong and courageous. Do not be frightened, for the LORD your God is with you." — Joshua 1:9
"While bodily training is of some value, godliness is of value in every way." — 1 Timothy 4:8
📖 Ch. 10 — Stay in Your Lane
"Youth sports work best when all the role players stay in their lanes."
Refs: responsibility to athletes and coaches — not parents.
Coaches: responsibility to athletes and parents — not refs.
Parents: responsibility to athletes — not coaches or refs.
⚠️ Don't Coach from the Stands
"'Why don't you shut up already?' — that's what a girl on the field finally screamed at her mom. Honestly, the rest of us had been wanting to say it too." (Ch. 10)

Sideline coaching confuses your athlete, competes with the actual coach, and robs them of the chance to figure things out on their own.
⚠️ Don't Argue with Refs
80% of youth sports officials quit after just two years. The reason? "The lack of respect for officials through the behavior of players, coaches, family members, and sports fans." (2024 Sport Journal study cited in Ch. 10)

When we run the best officials out of town, we're forced to replace them with inexperienced kids making minimum wage — and then we treat them worse.
✅ Do This Instead
Stay positive. Less is more. Limit in-game feedback to: "Great job!" "Keep it up!" "Way to go!"

Learn all the kids' names on your athlete's team — not just your kid. Using someone's name makes players feel seen, remembered, and valued.
Sports Missionary Identity (Ch. 10)
"If you are a Christian and you have kids in sports, whether you want to be or not, you are a sports missionary. God has placed you in the youth sports environment for this season of life."
Good missionaries develop a heart for people. They get curious about other parents' lives. They create space and normalize the non-superficial — even in the stands.
⚠️ The Car Ride — Danger Zone
"The car ride home is the silent killer of a kid's desire to play sports." — Nick Buonocore, Reformed Sports Project

The 'Four Horsemen of the Car Ride Home Apocalypse': silence, anger, irritation, and defensiveness. They show up when we try to teach at the worst possible moment.
Three Things Only (Ch. 10)
1. "I love you."
2. "I'm proud of you."
3. "I enjoyed watching you play — and I hope you had fun."
That's it. The car is not a classroom. Ask: "Is there anything from the game you want to talk about?" They'll almost always say no. That's okay. Turn on music.
📖 Peak-End Rule (Ch. 10)
"People recall experiences based on how they felt at the peak moment and the final moment. The car ride home is often the final moment. Make it safe."
📖 End of Day — Bedtime (Ch. 10)
"Bedtime is often when kids most want to talk — when the emotions have faded. Ask for permission before bringing up something difficult."
"Something happened today I want to talk about. Can I tell you? If not, when would be a good time in the next 24 hours?"
"When such and such happened — why did you do that? Tell me more."
"Can I share a story of when I was playing and something similar happened to me?"
"One of the values we're committed to as a family is ___. What does that look like in the context of sports?"
📖 Weekly Rhythm (Ch. 10)
"Find a consistent time each week to connect with your kids. For them, it's doughnuts and Dad. For me, it's discipleship."
Brian Smith takes each of his kids out for "Doughnuts with Dad" before school — one kid per day. If sports gives him a teaching moment, the doughnut shop is where it happens.
